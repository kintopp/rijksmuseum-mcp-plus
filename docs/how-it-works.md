### How it works

When you ask a question in your chat session, the AI assistant reasons about your query and decides which combination of the MCP server's [tools] is most likely to give you a useful answer drawn from the museum's [metadata]. It then evaluates the initial response and may trigger further rounds of queries until it is satisfied (the so-called 'agentic loop'). The MCP tools connect to different parts of the Rijksmuseum's data infrastructure: most queries go directly to the [vocabulary] database built from a periodic harvest of the museum's collection records while and concept-based queries are routed through a database of precomputed embeddings for semantic similarity search. Iconclass — the standard classification system for art subject matter — is available as a separate queryable resource. The results come back as structured data, which the AI then interprets, contextualises, and presents to you in natural language, often alongside an artwork displayed in an interactive deep-zoom image viewer. At each step, the AI can combine what it retrieves with its own background knowledge — about artists, periods, iconographic traditions, and historical context — to go beyond what the museum's metadata alone would tell you. That said, AI assistants trained for tool use tend to lean heavily on what the connected resources provide.

Because rijksmuseum-mcp+ has no direct access to the database driving the museum's [own search portal], it draws its answers from three different sources: a public API to the collections provided by the Rijksmuseum, its own databases of  metadata harvested from the museum's Linked Open Data and OAI-PMH interfaces, and the public Iconclass data. On the one hand, this is complicated — the AI assistant often needs to route its queries through multiple tools and data sources to answer a single question. And a small subset of queries possible on the museum's website are not possible here (e.g. sorting by popularity, browsing by exhibition). On the other hand, because rijksmuseum-mcp+ maintains its own copy of Rijksmuseum metadata, we can store, enrich and organise this data in multiple new ways ways aid research on the collections. For example:

- geocoding toponyms with existing place identifiers, then using this information to offer proximity and region-based search
- indexing curated metadata not exposed through the museum's search interface (descriptions, inscriptions, provenance, credit lines, and curatorial narratives)
- harvesting creator biographies with birth/death years and gender for demographic filtering
-  generating embeddings across 832,000 artworks for semantic search by meaning and concept. 

In short, the rijksmuseum-mcp+ trades off the conceptual simplicity and direct control of a traditional search interface against the more powerful and varied exploration modalities made possible through the mediation of an AI assistant.
