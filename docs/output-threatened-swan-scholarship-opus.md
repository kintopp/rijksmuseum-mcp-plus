### How the Interpretation Has Changed Over Time

The scholarship reveals a fascinating **multi-layered interpretive history** that mirrors broader shifts in art-historical method:

#### 1. Original Context: A Naturalistic Animal Painting (c. 1650)
Jan Asselijn painted the work around 1650 — an enormous canvas (144 × 171 cm) showing a swan fiercely defending its nest of eggs against a dog. Asselijn, primarily known as an Italianate landscape painter, died in 1652. **Steland-Stief's 1971 monograph** places the painting within his oeuvre as an unusually monumental animal study. At the time of creation, there is no evidence it was intended as political allegory — Asselijn died two decades before the events the painting was later said to depict.

#### 2. Retroactive Political Allegory (post-1672)
The painting's inscriptions — which are **later additions, not original** — transform the image into a political allegory of the *Rampjaar* (Disaster Year of 1672):
- **The swan** is labeled *"de Raad-Pensionaris"* (the Grand Pensionary = **Johan de Witt**)
- **The egg** is labeled *"Holland"*
- **The dog** is labeled *"de viand van de staat"* (the enemy of the state)

This reading connects the swan to De Witt's defense of the Dutch Republic against the invading forces of England, France, and the bishoprics of Cologne and Münster — all of which are tagged in the museum's metadata as associated places. De Witt was brutally murdered by an Orangist mob in August 1672. **R. van Luttervelt's** article specifically traces how the painting became a memorial to the De Witt brothers within the Rijksmuseum. The museum's own full title acknowledges the duality: *"The Threatened Swan; Interpreted later as an Allegory on Johan de Witt."*

#### 3. The Painting as National Symbol and Museum Foundation (1800–1810)
A major strand of scholarship — **Grijzenhout (1984 & later), the *Oud Holland* 1981 article, and Bergvelt et al.** — focuses on the painting's extraordinary institutional significance. When the **Nationale Kunstgalerij** (forerunner of the Rijksmuseum) opened in The Hague in **1800**, *The Threatened Swan* was its **very first acquisition**. It was purchased precisely *because* of its allegorical reading — a politically resonant image for a newly established national collection. Grijzenhout's *Een Koninklijk Museum* further traces the painting's prominence under **Louis Napoleon** (1806–1810), when the collection moved to Amsterdam.

The provenance trail — explored by **C.J. de Bruyn Kops** — traces the painting through the collection of the Amsterdam collector **Jan Gildemeester Jansz** before it entered national hands.

#### 4. Iconographic and "Hidden Meanings" Analysis (late 20th c.)
**Annemarie Vels Heijn et al.** in *Verborgen verhalen* (Hidden Stories) situated the painting within the broader Dutch tradition of embedding symbolic and allegorical meanings in seemingly naturalistic images, treating it as a case study of how meanings accrete over time — questioning whether the allegory is "in" the painting or projected onto it.

#### 5. Material and Conservation Studies (21st c.)
**Hubert Baija's** study on the frame (*'Verengeweld en kwabornamentiek'*) represents a shift toward material culture, examining the painting's authentic *kwab* (auricular) style frame as an object of study in its own right. **Lisanne Wepler's** dedicated monograph (published in both Dutch and English) is the most comprehensive single study, synthesizing the painting's artistic, political, institutional, and material histories.

### Summary: The Arc of Interpretation

| Period | Dominant Reading |
|--------|-----------------|
| **c. 1650** (creation) | Monumental animal painting; naturalistic drama |
| **Post-1672** | Retrofitted as political allegory of Johan de Witt defending the Republic |
| **1800** | National symbol — chosen as the founding artwork of the Dutch national collection |
| **20th century** | Art-historical debate: original intention vs. later allegorical layering |
| **21st century** | Material culture studies (frame); comprehensive monographic treatment; multimedia interpretation for visitors |

The painting's scholarship thus tells a double story: the history of a canvas, and the history of a nation's need to see itself reflected in art. The Rijksmuseum itself acknowledges this explicitly by maintaining the painting's full title as *"The Threatened Swan; **Interpreted later** as an Allegory on Johan de Witt"* — a rare curatorial admission that the most famous "meaning" of a masterpiece is a posthumous construction.
