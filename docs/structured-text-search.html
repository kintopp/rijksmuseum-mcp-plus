Searching the catalogue's words with precision

The catalogue's free-text records — object descriptions, curatorial narratives, and the inscriptions written on the works themselves — can now be searched by combining words with logic: requiring or excluding terms, matching exact phrases, finding words that sit close together, and catching every spelling of a word. You describe what you are looking for in ordinary language; the assistant composes the precise search.
A guide for researchers and curators · updated 2026-06-13
In brief
A catalogue's most revealing details often live in its prose — a cataloguer's description, a curator's wall text, the Latin abbreviation on a print's plate. Ordinary keyword search treats whatever you type as a single literal string to match. The catalogue can now do more: search its written records the way you would actually reason about them — this term or that one, this but not that, these two words near each other, every spelling of a stem. You never write the search yourself; you ask a question, and the assistant builds it.

What was missing, and why it matters

Until recently, a text search matched your words literally and as a whole. Type paintings of cats and dogs and the catalogue looked for that exact run of words — it did not read and as a request for either animal. For a single distinctive phrase this is fine. For the way a researcher actually frames a question, it quietly fails.

An analogy
Picture a very literal-minded reading-room assistant. You ask for "paintings of cats and dogs," hoping to see works with either animal. They don't hear and as logic — they go looking for something titled, word for word, "paintings of cats and dogs." Helpful, but only if you already know the exact words on the page.

Four kinds of question simply could not be expressed that way — and each is a question researchers ask all the time:

  • Either / or, and / not. "Water lilies or nymphéas, but not by Monet."
  • Either / or across different fields. A term phrased one way in the description or a different way in the wall text — capturing both, not only works that happen to use both.
  • Words near one another. Two terms appearing together in the same passage, not merely each somewhere in a long entry.
  • Every spelling of a stem. One search for sculpsit, sculptor, sculp. at once — essential where the same idea was abbreviated many ways.

The catalogue's simpler text filters — one for the description, one for inscriptions, one for the curatorial narrative — could only be combined with a blunt and (every term must appear, in whichever field), and a separate meaning-based search ("find works about this idea") covers a great deal of ground but cannot demand a precise arrangement of words. What was absent was the precise, composed middle ground. That is what the searches below provide.

The three bodies of text it searches

Three quite different kinds of writing sit in the catalogue, and their differences are exactly why composed searches help: a single subject may be named one way in one field and another way — or another language — in the next.

RecordCoverageCharacter
Descriptionabout half the collectionDutch, factual — what is shown, the materials, the recorded facts about signature and date
Curatorial narrativea few thousand worksMostly English, interpretive — meaning, theme, and the story behind the work
Inscriptionabout half the collectionLiteral transcription — the words actually written on the object, in whatever language and abbreviation the maker used

Three questions it now answers

Each example below is the kind of question that the simpler filters and the meaning-based search genuinely cannot reach. In every case you would simply ask the assistant in plain language; the indented box marked "what the assistant builds" is shown only for readers curious about the mechanics — it is not something you type.

Example 1 · either / or across fields

One event, recorded two different ways

"Find works concerning the Beeldenstorm — the 1566 wave of iconoclasm — whether a Dutch cataloguer called it beeldenstorm in the description, or an English curator called it iconoclasm in the wall text."

The two bodies of text describe the same event in different words and different languages, and the works they cover barely overlap. Requiring the term in both fields returns almost nothing; what you want is the union of the two — either phrasing, in either field. (Here we also set aside anything catalogued as a general "history" piece, to keep the focus on the event itself.)

What the assistant builds
match EITHER
  · "beeldenstorm" in the description
  · "iconoclasm" / "iconoclastic" in the curatorial narrative
and exclude anything titled "geschiedenis" (history)
Example 2 · words near one another

Signed and dated, in the same breath

"Find paintings the museum describes as both signed and dated together — the strongest catalogue statement that a work is genuinely by the artist's own hand."

A description that mentions a signature in one sentence and a date in another is weaker evidence than one asserting both at once — "gesigneerd … en gedateerd 1749." Asking the two Dutch words to appear close together drops the loose coincidences and keeps the strong claims. Neither the simple filters nor the meaning-based search can require that two words sit near each other.

What the assistant builds
in the description, find "gesigneerd" and "gedateerd"
within a few words of each other
Example 3 · every spelling, plus and / or

Who made the print — designer, engraver, publisher

"Old prints name their makers on the plate in Latin: who designed it (invenit), who engraved it (sculpsit / fecit), who published it (excudit). Find prints that record all three roles."

Each role was written many ways — sculpsit, sculptor, Sculpt., sculp. — because engravers abbreviated to fit the margin, so searching for a stem catches far more works than any single exact spelling. The catalogue's tidy "production role" labels cannot help here: they normalise everything into a few English terms, losing the original Latin and obscuring the cases where one person filled two roles ("sculptor et excudit"). The inscription is the primary source; this reads it directly.

What the assistant builds
in the inscription, require all three:
  · a designer  — stem "inven…" / "delineav…"
  · an engraver — stem "sculp…" / "incid…", or the word "fecit"
  · a publisher — stem "excud…"

How you would actually use it

You never compose any of the searches above by hand. You put your question to the assistant in ordinary language — "find prints that name a designer, an engraver, and a publisher in Latin" — and it works out the precise combination of terms, fields, phrases, and word-stems on your behalf. The result is a list of matching works you can open and examine like any other search. It runs on the catalogue's existing full-text records: nothing new to install, and the familiar plain keyword search is untouched and still available whenever a simple lookup is all you need.

In short
The catalogue's written records hold the details that distinguish one work from another — a cataloguer's phrasing, a curator's reading, a maker's abbreviation on the plate. Searching them now mirrors how researchers reason about them: either this or that, this but not that, these words together, every spelling of a stem. The three examples — one event recorded in two languages, the strong "signed and dated" claim, and the Latin maker's marks on a print — each reach works that nothing else in the toolkit can. And you reach them by asking a question, not by learning a syntax.
For implementers. These searches are compiled from a small structured request (textQuery) into SQLite FTS5 queries over the existing full-text indexes; the user's words are always quoted and the logical operators are generated server-side, so arbitrary query text can never reach the engine.
